# Day 27: 🎣 Phishing Awareness & Real-World Experience

## Overview

This document covers real-world experience identifying and handling **phishing attempts** — specifically email scams impersonating trusted companies like Apple to steal personal credentials or money.

---

## What Is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where cybercriminals send fraudulent emails, texts, or messages pretending to be from a legitimate, trusted organisation. The goal is to trick the recipient into:

- Handing over sensitive information (e.g. Apple ID credentials, passwords)
- Sending money (e.g. purchasing gift cards)
- Clicking malicious links or downloading malware

---

## Real-World Experience

### Case 1: Fake Apple ID Credential Theft

**What happened:**
Individuals received emails impersonating Apple, claiming there was an issue with their account. The emails prompted them to click a link and enter their **Apple ID username and password**.

**Red flags identified:**
- The sender's email address did not match an official Apple domain (`@apple.com`)
- The link in the email redirected to a suspicious, non-Apple URL
- Poor grammar and generic greetings like *"Dear Customer"* instead of the user's name
- A sense of urgency — threatening account suspension

**Outcome:** The individuals were helped to recognise the scam before entering any details. No credentials were compromised.

---

### Case 2: Gift Card Scam

**What happened:**
Victims received emails pretending to be from Apple (or an Apple representative), requesting that they purchase **gift cards** and share the redemption codes as a form of payment.

**Red flags identified:**
- Legitimate companies **never** request payment via gift cards
- The email used pressure tactics, claiming urgency or secrecy
- The request was entirely unusual and out of context

**Outcome:** The scam was caught before any gift cards were purchased. The individuals were advised and educated on this common tactic.

---

## How to Verify a Suspicious Email

Before taking any action on a suspicious email, run through this checklist:

| Check | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| **Sender's email address** | Does it match the real company's official domain exactly? |
| **Links in the email** | Hover over links — do they lead to the real website? |
| **Tone & urgency** | Is it pressuring you to act quickly or secretly? |
| **Greeting** | Is it generic ("Dear Customer") rather than your name? |
| **Request type** | Is it asking for gift cards, wire transfers, or login details? |
| **Grammar & spelling** | Are there unusual errors for a professional company? |

---

## Actions Taken

### ✅ Marking Emails as Spam/Scam
Suspicious emails were marked as **junk/spam** within the email client. This helps:
- Protect the individual going forward
- Train the email provider's spam filters
- Prevent future emails from the same source reaching the inbox

### ✅ Reporting to Authorities & the Impersonated Company
Each phishing attempt was reported to the relevant parties:

- **Apple:** Phishing emails impersonating Apple can be forwarded to `reportphishing@apple.com`
- **Action Fraud (UK):** [www.actionfraud.police.uk](https://www.actionfraud.police.uk)
- **FTC (US):** [reportfraud.ftc.gov](https://reportfraud.ftc.gov)
- **NCSC (UK):** Forward suspicious emails to `report@phishing.gov.uk`

Reporting helps authorities track and shut down phishing campaigns, protecting other potential victims.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Slow down** — phishing relies on panic and urgency. Take a breath before clicking anything.
2. **Verify independently** — if in doubt, go directly to the company's official website or call them.
3. **Never share credentials via email** — no legitimate company will ask for your password by email.
4. **Gift cards are never a valid payment method** — this is always a scam.
5. **Report it** — reporting protects you and others.
